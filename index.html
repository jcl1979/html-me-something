Old Print Methods Rock!

This page explores Lithography, the first "modern" printing method
along with an older Intaglio printing technique. Not dicussed here, but
interesting none the less, is an ancient print method, Relief printing,
See Japanese woodblock printing for an example of relief prints.

Lithography

F.O.C. Darley. "'First Blow For Liberty.' To the Memory of the Patriots of 1775." New York: Mason Bros, 1858. Engraving by A.H. Ritchie.

Lithography was invented in 1796 by German author and actor Alois Senefelder,
and originally used an image drawn on smooth limestone with oil, fat, or wax and
then tranferred to paper. It was a relatively inexpensive process that allowed for
"mass production" of artwork, literature, and cartography. A process so
effective that a similar method is used to fabricate electric circuits today!

Chromolithography

1893 Kurz & Allison "BATTLE OF WILSON'S CREEK" 76 & 78 Wabash, Chicago, Il

Chromolithography includes all types of lithography that are printed in color and
became the most successful method of color printing by the 19th century. Until the
introduction of chromolithographs, black and white prints were hand painted with watercolor.
Although chromos could be mass-produced, it took about three months to draw colors onto
the stones and another five months to print a thousand copies.

Mezzotints

George Caleb Bingham Stump Speaking New York 1858

Mezzotint is a printmaking process of the intaglio family. It was the first tonal
method to be used, enabling half-tones to be produced without using line or dot-based
techniques like hatching, cross-hatching or stipple. Mezzotint achieves tonality by
roughening the plate with thousands of little dots made by a metal tool with small teeth,
called a "rocker." In printing, the tiny pits in the plate hold the ink when the face
of the plate is wiped clean. A high level of quality and richness in the print can be achieved.

Maps are Sexy!

Johnson & Ward Map of Missouri and Kansas 1862, New York

One of the most practical applications of lithography/chromolithography was the
production of relatively inexpensive and accurate maps for the masses. They could
literally open up the world in a way that until then, was only available to the wealthy
and the military.
